2.1.9 Sorting algorithms questions

Practise bubble sort, merge sort and tracing passes, comparisons, swaps, splitting and merging.

1) A sports app needs to order a short list of race times into ascending order. Describe how a bubble sort would order the list. (4 marks)

Mark scheme

1 mark for each correct point:

  • Adjacent items are compared.
  • Items are swapped if they are in the wrong order.
  • The process is repeated over several passes through the list.
  • After each pass, a large value moves towards the end of the list.
  • The algorithm stops when the list is sorted or no swaps are needed.
  • Accept any other valid point.

2) A bubble sort is applied to 5, 2, 4, 1. Trace the first pass and state the list after that pass. (4 marks)

Mark scheme

Award up to 4 marks:

  • Compares 5 and 2 and swaps them to give 2, 5, 4, 1.
  • Compares 5 and 4 and swaps them to give 2, 4, 5, 1.
  • Compares 5 and 1 and swaps them to give 2, 4, 1, 5.
  • States that the list after the first pass is 2, 4, 1, 5.
  • Accept any other valid trace.

3) A shop system needs to sort a longer list of product prices. Describe how merge sort orders a list. (4 marks)

Mark scheme

1 mark for each correct point:

  • The list is split into smaller sublists.
  • The splitting continues until the sublists contain single items or very small lists.
  • The sublists are merged back together in order.
  • The final merged list is sorted.
  • Merge sort uses a divide and conquer approach.
  • Accept any other valid point.

4) A merge sort is applied to 6, 2, 8, 3. Trace the splitting and merging stages at GCSE level. (4 marks)

Mark scheme

Award up to 4 marks:

  • Splits the list into 6, 2 and 8, 3.
  • Splits into single-item lists: 6, 2, 8 and 3.
  • Merges 6 and 2 into 2, 6.
  • Merges 8 and 3 into 3, 8.
  • Merges 2, 6 and 3, 8 into 2, 3, 6, 8.
  • Accept any other valid trace.

5) A teacher wants to sort a short class list and a much longer whole-school list. Compare bubble sort and merge sort at GCSE level for these tasks. (4 marks)

Mark scheme

1 mark for each correct point:

  • Bubble sort repeatedly compares and swaps adjacent items.
  • Merge sort splits the list and then merges the parts in order.
  • Bubble sort is simpler to trace for short lists.
  • Merge sort is generally more efficient for larger lists.
  • Both algorithms produce a sorted list.
  • Accept any other valid point.
